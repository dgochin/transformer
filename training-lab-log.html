Lab Log · Part Two · Training

Two small models, and the weight of a harder story

Having built a clean, graded corpus, the next step was to train models on it and see what they learned. Two levels, two models, the same 14-million-parameter network, a measurable difficulty gap, and an honest lesson: a good loss number did not buy reliably coherent stories.

Model · 13.88M params, from scratch Hardware · one RTX 4060 Ti Result · right register, unreliable coherence

1 The network

A hand-written GPT, small enough to understand

No framework wrapper. The model is a compact GPT written by hand: multi-head self-attention, residual blocks with pre-layer-norm, tied input and output embeddings, and the GPT-2 scaled initialisation. Small enough to read end to end, which was the point.

The corpus was tokenised once into a packed binary stream (each story followed by an <|endoftext|> marker so the model learns where stories begin and end), then memory-mapped during training so start-up was instant and nothing was re-tokenised between runs.

WHY The Chinchilla rule of thumb suggested a model of barely one to two million parameters for this much data. TinyStories showed that rule is far too cautious for a narrow domain: coherent story models have run from one to thirty-five million parameters on comparable text. Fourteen million was chosen to sit comfortably inside that range, then validation loss was watched to confirm it wasn't overfitting.

2 Level 1

The first model learned to tell a story

Level 1 is toddler-core: one character, one object, one simple goal, resolved. Trained on 206,000 clean stories (23.3 million tokens), the model's loss fell fast and cleanly, and train and validation stayed locked together the whole way, the signature of a model that is learning the language rather than memorising the corpus.

The real test is not the loss but the text. Prompted with four opening cues, the model generated its own stories from scratch, and the results were a useful reality check. Of the four, one was clearly coherent, two were short but acceptable, and one drifted into near-nonsense:

L1 samples · generated · honest read Mixed. The best of the four held together as a real little story with a character and a goal. The other three ranged from short-but-fine to disjointed. The model had plainly learned the register and vocabulary, its sentences are simple, correct, and on-tone, but sustained, multi-sentence coherence was hit and miss.

This is the honest and more useful result. A validation loss of 1.40 is a genuinely good number, and it did not guarantee coherent text. The model learned the surface of the language convincingly while still fumbling the through-line of a story on half the prompts. Low loss bought fluency, not reliable sense.

NOTE Two caveats sit behind that gap, and they matter for what comes next. The loss was still falling when training stopped, so the model may have been undertrained rather than at its ceiling. And 14 million parameters is genuinely small. The next experiment holds training fixed and grows the model, to see which of the two was the real limit.

3 Level 2

Then a harder level, and the same honest picture

Level 2 adds a second character and an interaction between them, plus feelings that must have a stated cause ("she was sad because the kite broke"). The stories run longer, about 148 tokens against Level 1's 117. The open question: can a 14-million-parameter model hold two distinct characters together?

Trained the same way, on 150,000 stories (22.2 million tokens, all written by the API generator). At its best, the model did produce genuine two-character stories, distinct names, an interaction, an emotion with a cause. But like Level 1, it was inconsistent: the good samples were good, and the weak ones lost the thread or blurred the two characters together.

L2 samples · generated · honest read Capable at its best, shaky on average. When it worked, two named children interacted and one affected the other, exactly the target register. When it did not, the second character faded or the middle wandered. The capacity for two-character stories was clearly present; the reliability was not yet there.

So the answer to the question is a qualified yes: a 14-million-parameter model can reach for two-character interaction, but at this size and training length it cannot do it dependably. That is a more precise finding than a simple pass or fail, and it sets up exactly the right next move.

4 The finding

The difficulty ladder is real, and you can measure it

Because both models were identical, same size, same architecture, same token budget, same training, the only variable was the difficulty of the text. And the harder level cost the model something measurable.

The two curves tell a small, satisfying story of their own. At the very first checkpoint, Level 2 is fractionally lower, its stories all come from one generator, so their surface patterns are uniform and easy to grab early. But once the model moves past surface statistics into actual structure, the harder content pulls Level 2's loss above Level 1's and keeps it there. The two levels cross over, and the gap holds to the end: about 0.1 bits per token.

The same model, given a harder kind of story, compresses it less well. The difficulty grading was not just a label on the data. The model could feel it.

FINDING A curriculum's levels can be validated, not just asserted. Train identical models on each level and compare their converged loss: if a level is genuinely harder, its loss sits measurably higher. Level 2 cost ~0.1 bits per token over Level 1, and a 14M model still had the capacity to handle it, which is a data point about how model size might need to scale as the levels get harder still.

5 What carried over

Principles from the training half

Loss is a difficulty meter

Identical models on different data turn converged loss into a measurement of how hard the data is. Two numbers, one real finding.

Watch the train-val gap

It stayed near 0.05 at both levels: the models learned the language, not the corpus. The clean, large corpus made overfitting a non-issue at this size.

Loss is not coherence

A good validation loss (1.40) still produced disjointed samples on half the prompts. Fluency and sense are different things; only reading the output reveals the second.

Capacity is the open question

14M learned the register but not reliable coherence. The next run grows the model with everything else held fixed, to separate "too small" from "undertrained".

Next: grow the model, hold everything else fixed, and re-read the samples. Is the shaky coherence a limit of size, or was the model simply undertrained? One experiment separates the two.
